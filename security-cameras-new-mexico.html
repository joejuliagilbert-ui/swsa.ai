Google Nest Cameras (High-Signal Features)

If you are already in the Google ecosystem, Google Nest cameras and doorbells are a strong option because they prioritize meaningful alerts over constant motion noise.

  • Smart object detection: can distinguish people, vehicles, animals, and packages (supported models), which reduces false alerts.
  • Activity zones: alerts can be limited to specific areas like a driveway, porch, or gate rather than the entire frame.
  • Event-based history: recordings can be filtered by event type (for faster review and less scrolling).
  • Smart home integration: works well with Google Home app and Nest displays for quick viewing and announcements.

Note: some advanced features require a Nest Aware subscription (event history length and certain intelligent alerts).

What Most Homeowners Actually Need

Most homes do not need cameras everywhere. The highest ROI coverage usually starts with: front entry, driveway, garage access, backyard access points, and any side gates or hidden approaches. From there, you expand based on blind spots and daily routine.

  • Front entry coverage (doorbell camera or front-facing outdoor camera)
  • Driveway visibility (approach and vehicle area)
  • Garage access (door and side access points)
  • Backyard and gate access (common break-in routes)
  • Low-light considerations (usable video after dark)

Doorbell Cameras

Doorbell cameras are a common first step because they create visibility and deterrence at the main entry point. A good setup includes correct angle, usable night visibility, and notifications that do not become overwhelming.

  • Visitor alerts and front-door awareness
  • Package visibility and delivery monitoring
  • Placement and angle planning for usable video

Outdoor Security Cameras

Outdoor cameras provide broader perimeter awareness. The focus is usable sightlines and positioning that matches approach paths.

  • Driveways and vehicle areas
  • Yards, gates, and side paths
  • Garages and outbuildings
  • Back doors and sliding doors

Indoor Cameras (When Appropriate)

Indoor cameras are not needed for every home, but they can help in certain situations: common areas, entry hallways, or areas where you want event confirmation.

  • Common areas where events would be visible
  • Entry hallways as a secondary confirmation layer
  • Selective placement based on household comfort

Camera Placement Planning (The Difference Maker)

Placement matters more than the number of cameras. A good plan reduces false alerts and increases usable video.

  • Sightlines that capture faces and approach paths
  • Height and angle chosen for usable video
  • Lighting and night performance considerations
  • Alert configuration to reduce noise

FAQ – Security Cameras in New Mexico

How many cameras do I need?

Most homes start with 1–3 cameras covering front entry, driveway, and a key backyard or side access point. We expand based on blind spots.

Should I start with a doorbell camera or an outdoor camera?

Many homeowners start with a doorbell camera for front entry visibility. If the driveway or yard approach is more important, an outdoor camera may come first.

Can you help me choose placements before install?

Yes. Placement planning is one of the highest value parts of a camera system.
